## Post 2 - June 5, 2026
**Topic:** Marketers' new moral stance

Marketers' new moral stance is never gating content. I gated every resource I have, and 2 months later my newsletter sits at 3,8k subscribers, 43% open rate and 8.9% click rate
Some context: At distinctiva.io we write newsletters for clients every week, and I co-run theworkflow.digital with Sara. 

But mine sat on the to-do list way too long, until I finally launched it on April 5th. 

The girl finally ate her own dog food.

What's driving it:

1. Every resource sits behind an email gate. And I don't apologize for it.

I put out content I genuinely work hard on to help others: 5-7 times a week on LinkedIn, once a week on YouTube, every 2 weeks through Substack for The Workflow. All free. I won't apologize for gating one resource.

Because it's not a random PDF I whipped up in an afternoon. Every resource I gate is heavily researched, carefully built, and something I actually use myself. If it took me 20 hours to build, an email is a fair trade.

2. The newsletter is where I do my deepest thinking.

Most of my content flywheel starts in the newsletter or a YouTube script, because that's where I'm not fighting character limits. Step by step processes, screenshots, full workflows, nothing held back.

I don't send an issue unless it's been properly thought out. If I have nothing worth sending, I don't send.

3. Subject lines are hooks. I treat them exactly like LinkedIn hooks.

"I studied 131 hooks from creators who are actually growing" pulled a 44% open rate. Same psychology, different inbox.

4. When I sell, I just sell.

No burying the offer under five "value points." My promotional issues are direct: here's the community, here's the price, here's the deadline.

Result: 101 paid members in my community, and 80% of them came through email.

When you're useful, be the most useful. When you sell, go for the sale. Mixing the two weakens both.
